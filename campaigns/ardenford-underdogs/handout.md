# Ardenford Underdogs — Player Handout

> A handful of no-names with empty pockets, one cheap job, and a city built on top of a graveyard nobody's finished robbing yet.

You live in **Ardenford**, the capital city of **Ardenhaven** — a small country on the recovering side of a long war against a much larger southern empire. Ardenhaven is a constitutional monarchy: a council of lords, guilds, and temples runs the realm together, more or less. Money is tight. People are tired. People also keep going.

The whole story takes place in and below this one city.

## Quick facts

- **The current age of the world:** the Age of Trade. The wars are over and coin runs everything now.
- **The current date:** Year 3 of the Age of Trade (written ⋈3 in scholarly notation).
- **Where you live:** Ardenford, capital of Ardenhaven. The city is built on top of, and into, the ruins of an older civilization that collapsed roughly 4,000 years ago.
- **Your starting level:** 1.
- **Tone:** grounded, classical D&D. The monsters are real, the danger is real, but it isn't grimdark. People still drink, gamble, and laugh. The dungeons just *also* kill them.

## The premise

In Ardenford, the way you become *somebody* is by going underground.

The city sits on the bones of a dead empire. The Ancients — whoever they were — left behind ruins, vaults, sealed levels, and artifacts that still hum, sing, or kill. Above ground, the city has bridges, councils, and guilds. Below it, things get older fast. The bigger artifacts have made families rich. The lesser ones have funded a whole working class of *delvers* — adventuring crews who go in, take what they can carry, and try to make it back out.

Several established crews already work the city's ruins. They have names, contracts, equipment, regular drink-tabs at the right taverns. **You don't.**

You are a handful of people who can't get hired by any of them.

You're new. You don't have a reputation. You don't have a sponsor. You don't have a clean record, or you don't have *any* record. Maybe you're too poor to afford the gear deposits the better crews require. Maybe you're the wrong race in the wrong room. Maybe you turned someone down once and word got around. Whatever the reason, the established crews look right past you, and the only thing you have in common with the other people the city ignores is that you all need money by next week.

So you band together. Not because you trust each other yet. Because nobody else will work with you, and the rent is due.

## Where it happens

**Your hub: the Thar'kar Taproom.** Eastern edge of the city, dwarven-run. It isn't an inn — there are no rooms for rent — but it's where guides, porters, and underdog crews hang around looking for work. The route board behind the bar lists names, distances, warnings, and prices that change with the weather. If you don't have a sponsor, this is where jobs find you. The bar is the kind of place a desperate stranger walks into looking for the cheapest possible help.

**Other places in Ardenford you'll come to know:**

- **The Anvil & Oar** — gear and weapons.
- **Willowglass Apothecary** — potions, salves, healing supplies.
- **Silverbridge Arcana** — appraisals, identifications, the occasional scroll.
- **The Concordance Library** — research and lore. When you find something you don't understand, this is where you go.
- **Wayward Compass, Leafscript Cabinet, The Still Page** — supporting shops for maps, books, ink, paper, and the small things you forget to pack until you need them.

**The ruins.** Three known sites in Ardenhaven, plus sealed levels under the city itself.

- **The Western Scrubland Ruins** — the most picked-over and best-mapped. Where most low-level crews start.
- **The Deep Fall Ruins** — deeper, more dangerous, contested.
- **The Stone Breach Ruins** — newer access, less known.
- **Sealed sub-levels under Ardenford itself** — officially off-limits, occasionally accessible to crews willing to take a risk and pay a fine if caught.

## The other crews (you are not one of them)

These are the established names you'll hear in the city. Above ground, they're polite — even helpful — they'll buy you a drink, give you advice, sometimes warn you off a bad job. Below ground, in the ruins, the rules change. They will not always kill you for getting in their way. They might.

| Crew | Ethos | What they're like in the ruins |
|---|---|---|
| **The Black Ledger** | Lawful Evil | Predators with paperwork. Will sell anything, including cursed things. If you have something they want, you have a problem. |
| **The Inkbound Charter** | Lawful Neutral | Surveyors. Treat ruins like a job site. Will work *around* you cleanly if you don't mess with their grid. |
| **The Lantern Wardens** | Neutral Good | Rescuers. They charge — but they'll come pull you out of a hole if you can pay. The closest thing to allies the underdogs have. |
| **The Shardrunners** | Chaotic Neutral | Thrill-seekers. They'll race you to a find. Loud, flashy, in debt to dangerous people. |
| **The Compass Edge** | Mercenaries | Specialize in pulling other crews out of trouble — for a price. |

You'll meet them. You'll be hired by some, hunted by others, sometimes both in the same week. Your long game is to become a name like one of theirs.

## How a delve works

1. **Find a job.** Posted at Thar'kar, given by an NPC, found in a rumor.
2. **Pay for the dive.** Each delve costs a fixed amount of gold — food, lamp oil, rope, splints, bandages. If you can't afford it, you don't go. Your starting kit covers the first one.
3. **Go in. Find things. Get out.**
4. **Sell, appraise, or hold.** Splits happen on the way home. Big finds need a fence or a buyer. Cursed finds need a *careful* buyer.
5. **Spend.** Rent, gear upgrades, training, buying off the people you owe. Whatever's left funds the next dive.

If you make money, you can dive again next week. If you don't, you're back to scraping. There is no safety net.

## Tone

D&D-classical. Goblins are real, traps kill, the gods exist somewhere, magic is rare and noteworthy. The world isn't ironic. The danger isn't a joke. But it isn't relentlessly bleak either — Ardenford has festivals, taverns, gossip, weather, and people who like their lives. The horror is what's *under* the city, not the city itself.

When something kills you, it doesn't gloat. It just kills you. Bring rope.

## Making a character

You'll start at **level 1**. All standard D&D classes and ancestries are open — play what you want. Here's how the most common ones land in this region:

- **Common, nobody looks twice** — humans, dwarves, halflings, gnomes, elves.
- **Less common, still normal** — orcs (most often from the western **Garrok** lands, the open country to the west), tieflings, dragonborn.
- **Rare** — anything more exotic. You can still play it; just know that people will notice when you walk into a room. For an underdog crew, standing out can be a feature, not a problem.

For your background, answer one question: **why isn't a real crew willing to hire you?** Even one sentence is enough. Examples:

- *"My last crew disappeared in a ruin. People assume I sold them out."*
- *"I'm a deserter from somewhere I won't name."*
- *"I'm too young, my father's a known drunk, and I'm working my own name from zero."*
- *"I'm not from around here, and people don't trust what they don't recognize."*
- *"I broke a contract. The Black Ledger has my mark. Nobody legitimate will sign with me."*

## The wider city

You don't have to engage with city politics to play. But the politics exist. Ardenhaven is run by a Council of lords, guilds, and temples; there are district rivalries; there's an over-stretched militia; there are refugee quarters; and there are noble houses who quietly fund some of the bigger crews. If you start asking who really funds the Black Ledger, or who owns the sealed sub-levels under the city, or why the realm tolerates the whole ruin-raiding economy at all — those threads will pull back on you. Pull them if you want to. Or stay focused on the next delve. Both are fine.

## Your first job

A merchant has posted a low-pay, low-danger job at Thar'kar: retrieve a specific small item from a known-safe outer chamber of the Western Scrubland Ruins. The pay is bad. The job is beneath the established crews — they laughed at the posting and walked.

You see a few other people standing around the route board with the same desperate look you have. The dwarf behind the bar reads the names off the posted list, looks up at all of you, and says:

> *"Well? You're a crew now or you're not."*

You're a crew.
