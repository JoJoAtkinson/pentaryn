# The Bridgefire of Morvalos
*The burning of Harthen Bridge — held on the last night of the month of Morvalos*

> Every year they build it. Every year they burn it. That is the point.

## At a Glance

- **Where:** Ardenford and the Harthen Ford, a natural crossing on the old southern road roughly a half-mile below the city's Lower Tier
- **When:** The last night of Morvalos (the coldest calendar month), beginning at dusk, ending when the bridge collapses
- **Primary event:** The communal march from Ardenford down to Farrow Hill, the vigil, and the ritual burning of the bridge
- **Who attends:** A huge portion of the city — ten thousand or more in good years, smaller only when weather is genuinely dangerous
- **Purpose:** To remember the Burning at Harthen Ford (~3980 AF), to pledge they would do it again, and to put that pledge in fire instead of words
- **Cultural status:** One of Ardenford's most fiercely protected traditions; attempts to "modernize" it have triggered two formal censure votes against sitting consuls

## The Historical Origin

### The Battle of Harthen Ford

Roughly three centuries before the current year, a southern army pushed north along the old trade road in winter, counting on cold and the season to keep the city passive. They were not wrong about the cold. They were wrong about the people.

The defenders of early Ardenford fell back in organized retreat to the high ridge above the Harthen Ford — the last natural crossing before the approach roads climbed into the city. The ford was shallow enough to wade in summer. In midwinter, swollen with snowmelt, it was not. The wooden bridge was the only practical crossing for an army moving with supply wagons.

From the ridge, the defenders could see everything: the enemy columns advancing across the southern field below, the ford, the bridge, and the gap between them.

The bridge needed to burn. No one on the ridge could reach it without crossing the open field under enemy observation. The enemy was already moving toward it.

### The Runner

The accounts disagree on who went. Some say it was a soldier named Errath Voss, a runner by trade before the war. Some say it was a woman whose name was never recorded because the clerks thought she was just a camp follower. Some say a commander ordered it. Some say no order was given at all — someone just went.

What every account agrees on: a single person came off the ridge, crossed the open field at a dead run in full view of both armies, reached the bridge carrying fire, soaked whatever they could reach in whatever combustible they had, and put the torch to it.

The bridge burned. The enemy advance halted. The ford held.

The runner was cut off on the far side of a burning bridge with an army between them and home. The accounts disagree on what happened next. Most say they didn't come back. The city has largely stopped arguing about this — the ending isn't what the festival is about.

The run is what the festival is about.

### Why It Became a Ceremony

In the years after, the people who had stood on that ridge and watched did something strange: they rebuilt the bridge. Not because anyone ordered them to. Not because it was strategically necessary. Because they couldn't stop thinking about what they'd seen.

The first burning was informal — a handful of veterans, the rebuilt bridge, a torch, a small crowd at midwinter. The next year, more people came. The year after that, the whole lower ward turned out. Within a generation it had become what it is now: the largest single gathering Ardenford produces — larger than elections, more emotionally potent than any government ceremony, and completely impossible to explain in a budget document.

## The Bridge Itself

### Rebuilding (All Year)

The Harthen Bridge is a low wooden span — wide enough for a cart, long enough to cross the ford, nothing architecturally impressive. Every year, after the burning, volunteer labor begins the rebuilding. There is no government mandate, no tax allocation, no formal guild contract. The labor organizes itself: lumber donations from the foothills merchants, labor from whatever wards want to contribute, carpentry guidance from whoever knows wood joinery that year.

By autumn the bridge is complete. It serves as a real working crossing for the rest of the year — people use it daily for market runs and the southern trade road. This matters. It is not a prop. It is a working bridge that the community then agrees to destroy.

### Soaking Night (Eve of the Festival)

The night before the burning, a small designated crew — always volunteers, always people who have done it before and train the new ones — soaks the bridge in rendered tallow, pine resin, and oil. This is done in the dark, largely in silence, and is considered serious work rather than festive preparation. The bridge reeks of it for a full day beforehand.

Children are told you can smell when Morvalos night is close. They're right.

## The Festival: Dusk to Collapse

### Afternoon: The Ward Candles

The ceremony doesn't begin at the ford. It begins in the city.

In the hours before dusk, each ward lights a single large candle in its central square — one per neighborhood, placed in the open where anyone can see it. These are not decorative. They are the start of the chain. Each family that intends to march lights a hand-candle from the ward candle before they leave.

By the time the sun drops, the city is full of moving lights. Individuals, families, whole working crews still in their trade clothes, militia in off-duty dress, merchants who closed early, children carried on shoulders. Everyone moving in the same direction. Everyone carrying a flame.

The streets funnel naturally because of Ardenford's terrain — switchback roads, narrow approaches between tiers — and what this does to ten thousand people descending at once is something first-timers are never prepared for. The sound is low: footsteps, quiet conversation, the wind. No drums. No horns. Just the collective noise of a city in motion and the flutter of ten thousand small flames trying not to go out in the cold.

Locals cup their candles. Strangers learn to do the same. Keeping your flame burning is a small private task that keeps people's heads down and focused inward for the whole walk. There's something in that. Nobody planned it that way. It turned out that way anyway.

### The Road South

Once through the Lower Tier, the procession moves south on the old trade road — the same road the enemy came up three centuries ago, now walked in the opposite direction. The road drops out of the city proper into open, frost-hardened fields. The Harthen Ford comes audible before it's visible. Farrow Hill appears on the right as the road curves: not tall, two hundred feet at most, but commanding — it overlooks the ford, the flat southern field below, and on clear nights, the dark smear of the treeline in the distance.

The hill holds as many people as can fit. The front of the march reaches the top while the back is still leaving the city. From above, the procession looks like a slow river of candlelight flowing down a road and pooling on a hillside.

### The Vigil

There is no formal crowd structure on the hill. People find their own ground and stand with who they came with, or find themselves beside strangers. Merchants next to dockworkers next to militia next to the kind of elderly person who has made this walk for sixty years and intends to do it again next year.

At the center high point stands the city's designated speaker for that year — not a government official by tradition, but someone chosen by informal community consensus: a veteran some years, a ward captain, occasionally a craftsperson or innkeeper with nothing official to their name except that the city decided to trust them with this. The speaker reads the account of the battle. Not an interpretation. One of the four surviving historical versions, read in full, in the old phrasing, without commentary.

People who have heard it a hundred times still go quiet.

After the reading, the hill goes silent. This is the vigil. No fixed length. It ends when the runner appears.

### The Runner

The runner's identity is kept private until after the burning. The selection is informal — the previous year's runner, working with ward captains and veterans' representatives, chooses the next. There is no public application. No prize. The honor is doing it.

The runner trains for the field crossing. It is farther than it looks from the hill, and it is done in cold and in dark, carrying an open flame over frost-hardened ground. Every runner has been warned about ankle holes in the frozen earth. Most have found at least one in training.

At some unmarked moment — the exact time is never announced — a single figure appears at the far edge of the field below. Far enough that in darkness they are barely more than a moving shape. They are carrying fire.

They cross the open field at a run. Not a jog. A dead run. The same ground. The same destination. The same thing in hand.

Ten thousand people watch from the hill. No one speaks.

### The Burning

The runner reaches the bridge. On a clear night the crowd can see the moment of the throw — the torch arcing through the air, landing on oil-soaked wood. On foggy or overcast nights, only the fire announces it: a crack of light as the bridge catches, followed almost immediately by a roar as the resin and tallow pull the flame across the entire span at once.

The bridge goes up fast and full, and the light of it is visible from Ardenford's upper tiers. People who couldn't make the march watch from rooftops. People on the hill who have seen it a hundred times still flinch at how fast it goes.

The crowd watches until the bridge fails into the ford. No signal. No announcement. When there is nothing left to burn, people begin, quietly, to go home.

## Why Administrators Don't Understand It

The Bridgefire costs real money by anyone's honest accounting. Lumber. Labor. The final week of lost transit use. Cleanup of the ford afterward. The soaking crew's materials.

Every few decades someone in the Council of Consuls does the math and raises a hand. The public response is always the same: not a formal protest, not a petition, just a quiet and total refusal to engage, and the project of rebuilding the bridge continues without comment.

In 4251 AF, a consul proposed replacing the bridge burn with an illusion display — same sight, no cost, no labor, no real fire. The proposal was voted down informally by the population at large before it reached a formal vote. The consul was not reelected.

In 4289 AF, a different proposal suggested building a smaller "ceremonial bridge" instead of a full working span — cheaper, faster, same visual effect. The community labor crews began rebuilding that year's bridge louder and more publicly than usual. The proposal died.

What confuses administrators is that the most vocal defenders of the ceremony are not usually the nostalgic or the religious. They are as often as not practical working people who grumble about waste in other contexts and would not spend a plank on something useless. Those same people will give their own weekends to cutting lumber for the Harthen Bridge.

When pressed, the answer is always some version of the same thing:

*The fake version would be easier. The whole point is that we don't do the easy version.*

The city is not saying *we remember what happened*. It is saying *we would do it again*. You cannot say that with illusions.

## After the Burning

The dispersal is quiet. The return walk to Ardenford is less ordered than the march out, more dispersed, markedly less solemn. Candles gutter or get pocketed. People drift back in groups. The solemnity is spent.

By midnight the taverns are full, and the night becomes, for its remaining hours, a festival in the more familiar sense — food, warmth, drink, the particular ease that comes after a large collective thing has been completed together. The Wayward Compass and every inn in the Middle Tier pack tight. The talk is not heavy. What is left after the hill is relief.

The runner's name is announced before midnight. Some years there is a cheer. Some years just a round of drinks bought in that direction. No medal, no monument. Occasionally someone carves the name somewhere unofficial — a beam, a door post, a bridge plank laid early for next year's span. The city doesn't endorse this. Nobody stops it either.

## How Players Experience It

The march is the thing. Ten thousand people moving through the Lower Tier streets at dusk, every one of them carrying a lit candle, is something that has to be walked through to understand. Players who push to the front of the crowd on Farrow Hill will have a clear view of the field below. Players who stay in the body of the crowd will feel the weight of the city around them. Both positions are true.

The vigil is when NPCs show their character: who goes quiet, who watches the field, who watches the people. Torren Ashvale says nothing during the vigil. Selise Dawnquill has been heard to say something very quietly in a language she doesn't use publicly.

The burning is brief. It ends faster than newcomers expect. What follows tends to stay with players longer than the flame.

### Complications Worth Running

- The runner doesn't appear at the expected time. The crowd waits. The field below is dark and still.
- One of the ward candles in the procession is not what it appears — the wax is loaded with something, and whoever prepared it expected it to be carried into a crowd of ten thousand.
- The bridge burns. So does something downstream that was not supposed to be there.
- Two figures appear at the edge of the field. No one knows which is the runner.
- The bridge burns too quickly and too completely. The soaking crew cannot be found afterward.
- A name the party recognizes appears on one of the surviving historical accounts of the battle, in handwriting that doesn't match the document's age.

## Food of the Night

The food is working food — warm, portable, no ceremony required:

- **Tharkar Spit Bread:** Flat dense loaves off iron pans from Thar'kar's street cart, eaten walking
- **Ember Pork:** Salted pork slow-cooked all day, served in hand portions; Lower Tier specialty
- **Pine-Spiced Cider:** Near-universal on the march back; sold in ceramic cups from every tavern with a street window
- **Hardfrost Biscuits:** Dockworkers' food — unleavened, very shelf-stable, eaten without conversation
- **Ward Stew:** Whatever the neighborhood had that day, kept hot in a shared pot and ladled to whoever is still outside
